Adapix — a note for Dad

What I'm asking you to put your name on, in plain English

I need an adult to be the official account holder for one of the services my business runs on. Here's exactly what that means — what it costs, what you'd be responsible for, and what you wouldn't be.

From Rocco & Ben · Adapix (adapixai.com) · July 2026

What Adapix is

Ben and I are building Adapix — software that helps small businesses (plumbers, dentists, salons) follow up with customers who went quiet. It writes the follow-up text or email, and the business owner reads and approves every single message before it sends. The website is live at adapixai.com and the product works today.

What Twilio is, and why we need you

Twilio is the company that provides the phone numbers and text-messaging service our software sends through. It's a large, publicly traded US company — the same one apps like Uber and Airbnb use to send you texts.

To send texts to real customers, Twilio requires the account to be upgraded and registered with US phone carriers — and the account holder must be a legal adult. That's the only reason I can't do this part myself. Nothing about the business changes; it's whose name is on the account paperwork.

What you'd actually be doing

The 10-minute task On the Twilio upgrade form, you'd enter: your legal name, address, date of birth, possibly a photo of your ID (their identity check), and a credit or debit card with a $20 starting balance. Later there's a second short form ("A2P registration") that registers us with phone carriers so our texts aren't marked as spam — same information again plus a mobile number they text a code to.

What it costs

One-time starting balance (becomes usage credit, not a fee)$20
Carrier registration, one-time~$19
Monthly: phone number + carrier fee~$3–6 /mo
Per text message sent~1¢
Realistic total for our current size~$25 up front, then <$10/mo

I can pay these costs — the card just has to be an adult's. If you'd rather the charges go to me, we can top up the balance manually instead of enabling auto-recharge.

What you ARE and AREN'T signing up for

  • You are: the legal owner of our Twilio account — the adult of record. You'd get their emails (billing receipts, policy notices), and you could log in and see everything at any time.
  • You are: responsible that the account isn't used for spam or scams. It isn't and won't be — every message is a follow-up a real business owner personally approved, with opt-out honored. That's the entire design of our product.
  • You are not: signing a contract with a term or a cancellation fee. Twilio is pay-as-you-go; the account can be closed any time.
  • You are not: liable for the company itself — this is one vendor account, not ownership, debt, or anything tax-related beyond the small charges on the card.
  • You are not: going to be doing ongoing work. After the two forms, I operate everything day to day.

The honest risks

If our account ever broke Twilio's messaging rules, Twilio's recourse is to suspend or close the account — the practical consequence lands on the business (our texts stop), not on you personally. The financial exposure is capped by the balance on the card, and we can set a low auto-recharge cap (or none) so a bug can't run up a bill. Biggest realistic annoyance: an occasional verification email you'd forward to me.

One more thing, so it's said up front

Two other services will eventually need an adult of record too: Stripe (when we start charging customers — that one involves a bank account for payouts) and possibly our iMessage provider. I'm not asking for those today, but if you're comfortable being the adult of record generally, it keeps everything consistent and I won't have to ask twice.

Thanks, Dad. This is a real product with a real website and our first trial users coming — this one signature is what makes the "text" part of it actually work. Happy to sit with you while you fill it in, and you can watch every step. — Rocco
