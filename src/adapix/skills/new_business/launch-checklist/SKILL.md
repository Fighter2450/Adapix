# Launch checklist

The founder is past the deciding-what-to-do phase. They need a list of
the things that actually have to get done to open. Generic checklists
("set up an LLC, get an EIN") are useless; the founder can find those
themselves. Your job is to give them the *order-of-operations* specific
to their business type.

## Step 1 — Identify the launch shape

Different businesses launch differently. Pick the shape that fits:

- **Has-to-open-on-a-date** — restaurant, retail, salon, gym. Big bang
  with a deadline. Marketing matters before day one.
- **Soft-launch** — services, consulting, SaaS, online creator. Sell to
  one customer, then ten, then ramp. No public opening day.
- **Inventory-first** — e-commerce, makers, food brand. Get the product
  right before the storefront opens.
- **License-gated** — anything regulated (medical, legal, dispensary,
  childcare). Compliance is the long pole; sequence around it.

Don't ask which shape it is — infer from `practice_type_label` and what
the founder has told you.

## Step 2 — Generate the checklist

Produce 4 sections, each with 4-8 items. Item should be one line, action
verb first, concrete enough that the founder can DO it (not "think
about" it).

### 1. Legal + financial (the things that block everything else)

Be specific to the business type. Examples:

- Restaurant: food handler permit, health inspection, ABC license if
  serving alcohol, EIN, business bank account, sales tax permit
- Therapist: state licensure, malpractice insurance, HIPAA-compliant
  intake, NPI number, superbill template
- E-commerce: business entity formation, EIN, resale certificate,
  sales tax registration in home state, payment processor

### 2. Physical / digital footprint

Where the business exists for customers. Examples:

- Storefront: lease signed, signage ordered, POS chosen + installed,
  staff schedule for opening week
- Online: domain bought, hosting picked, payment processor, scheduling
  page if applicable, email forwarding set up

### 3. The first 10 customers

This is where most founders go vague. Push for specifics:

- One named person they will call THIS WEEK
- One channel they'll post to daily for the first 30 days
- One thing they'll give the first 10 customers (discount, gift, public
  thank-you)

### 4. Day-one essentials

Things that have to exist by the moment the doors open / the site goes
live:

- A working way to take a payment
- A working way for a customer to contact them back
- A working way to deliver the first sale
- A working way to know it happened (an email, a dashboard, a notebook)

## Step 3 — Identify the long pole

After producing the list, point at the one item that takes the longest
to get done (usually licensing or build-out) and say: *"This is the one
you should start tomorrow. Everything else can run in parallel."*

## Style

- Don't number the items inside a section unless the order strictly
  matters. Use bullet dashes.
- Don't include "make a business plan" or "do market research" —
  performative. Skip them.
- Skip the "celebrate your launch!" item. It's filler.
