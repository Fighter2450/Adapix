# Pricing strategy

Pricing is mostly about confidence and structure, not math. Most new
founders underprice because they imagine a customer saying no. Your job
is to pick a model that makes saying yes easy, then put a number on each
offering that the founder can defend.

## Step 1 — Pick the model

Walk through these in order. The first one that fits is usually the
right answer.

- **Per-unit** — they sell discrete things (haircuts, oil changes, lattes).
  Best for: trades, retail, food, repair, beauty.
- **Per-project** — work has a clear scope and endpoint. Best for: design,
  consulting, construction, photography, weddings.
- **Hourly** — used when scope is unpredictable and trust is high. Best
  for: legal, deep specialty consulting. Worst for: anything else.
- **Subscription** — ongoing access to the same thing. Best for: SaaS,
  cleaning services, lawn care, gym, content libraries.
- **Retainer** — monthly fee for ongoing availability + capped output.
  Best for: marketing, accounting, fractional roles.

If you're not sure, ask: *"Does the customer pay you once for a thing, or
keep paying you over time?"* Once → project or per-unit. Over time →
subscription or retainer.

## Step 2 — Put numbers on each offering

For each item in the catalog:

1. Ask the founder what they'd be **embarrassed to charge** — too low.
2. Ask what they'd be **terrified to charge** — too high.
3. The starting price is roughly the geometric mean (multiply them,
   take the square root). Round to a clean number.

Why this works: it forces the founder to confront both anchors instead of
defaulting to the floor.

## Step 3 — Add a price ladder, but only if it makes sense

If they have a lead offering, consider:

- A **starter** at ~50% of the lead price (smaller scope, lower commitment)
- A **premium** at ~2-3x the lead price (more scope, faster, more access)

Three tiers max. If they only have one offering, one price is fine.

## Numbers to anchor on (rough US 2025)

Cite these if the founder has no intuition:

- Skilled trade hour: $80–150 retail
- Solo consultant project: $2k–10k typical entry
- Boutique retainer (1 person of attention): $2k–6k/mo
- SaaS for SMBs: $29–199/mo per seat
- Local coffee drink: $4–6 average ticket
- Restaurant dinner ticket: $25–65 fast casual, $80–180 sit-down

## Don't do this

- Don't recommend "discount to acquire customers." Bad customers come in
  through discounts and they leave the moment the discount ends.
- Don't add a free tier unless the business is software with near-zero
  marginal cost.
- Don't price by working backwards from a salary goal — price from
  customer perceived value, then check if the volume gets them there.
