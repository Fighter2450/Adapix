# First-customer plan

The hardest 10 sales in a business's life are the first 10. After that
everything compounds. Most founders skip planning this step because
they're imagining customer 1,000, not customer 1. Your job is to bring
them back to 10.

## Step 1 — Make the list

Ask the founder: *"Open a doc. List 25 specific people, businesses, or
groups who might be customer 1-10. Real names. People you could text
today."*

Push back on these:

- "I'll run Facebook ads" → ads don't get the first 10, they scale
  what already works
- "I'll post on LinkedIn" → posting is not a customer list
- "Friends and family" → fine as a warm-up but those aren't real
  customers, they're a favor

If they can't make it to 25, that's the actual blocker. Their network
isn't big enough yet — see Step 4.

## Step 2 — Pick 3 channels

Out of every possible marketing channel, pick exactly 3 the founder will
focus on for the first 90 days. Match the channel to the business type
and to where the founder will actually show up:

- **Hyperlocal**: Nextdoor, local Facebook groups, in-person events,
  flyers in the relevant zip code. Best for: trades, services, food,
  retail.
- **Search**: Google Maps, Google Ads, SEO basics. Best for: anything
  someone Googles when they have a problem.
- **Referral programs**: explicit "send us a customer, get $X." Best
  for: service businesses with a clear value per customer.
- **Industry adjacency**: showing up where existing service providers
  already are. (Dentists know orthos; gyms know nutritionists.) Best
  for: professional services.
- **Content**: regular short-form content that demonstrates competence.
  Best for: consulting, coaching, anything sold to people who research
  before they buy.

Reject anything where the founder has zero existing presence. Channels
are stacks of effort; they only work if the founder shows up.

## Step 3 — Write the first outreach

Draft the actual message the founder will send to person #1 on their
list. It should:

- Address them by name
- Mention one specific thing about them or their business
- Make ONE small ask (a 15-minute call, a meeting, a referral)
- End with a specific time (Tuesday at 3? Thursday morning?)

Example:

```
Hi Maya — I'm finally opening the bakery on Carson next month.
I noticed you've been running tastings for new spots at the
coffee shop next door — would you be open to a 20-min coffee
Thursday morning? I want your read on doing a soft-open tasting,
and I'd like to make sure you get a free pastry box every Monday
in trade for honest feedback.
```

Don't write it for a hypothetical — write it for ONE actual person on
their list.

## Step 4 — If the list is too thin

If the founder can't get to 25 names, do these in order before any
marketing:

1. **Spend 2 weeks "in the building"** — show up at venues where their
   target customer already is. Coffee shops, gyms, industry events,
   neighborhood spots. Talk to people. No selling. Just presence.
2. **Reactivate dormant connections** — people they used to work with,
   neighbors, parents at their kids' school. One message each.
3. **Help someone else's customer first** — find an adjacent business
   and offer to help their customers in a way that brings the founder
   into contact with future customers of their own.

## Anti-patterns

- Don't recommend an MVP funnel. The founder doesn't have traffic yet,
  funnels are about conversion rates.
- Don't recommend brand consistency exercises until after customer 1.
- Don't recommend a logo iteration. The logo will not earn them a single
  customer. Move on.
